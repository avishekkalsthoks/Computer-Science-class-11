Chapter 8: Information Security and Cyber Law

1. What is Information Technology? Describe it with its social impacts.

Information Technology (IT) is a modern concept or methodology of communication. It is the combined form of Information and Communication Technology (ICT), which is widely used for information flow in many fields. IT has brought the world closer day by day, making it possible to send and receive messages across the globe within seconds. It is extensively used in various sectors, including education, industry, banks, research centers, hospitals, etc. IT enables distant learning, which has revolutionized education, and it is used in industries to control product quality and quantity. In hospitals, it helps in management and treatment processes. IT is also widely used in animation and entertainment sectors, and scientists use computers for research and experiments. Space technology has become possible due to IT. While IT has a positive impact on society, it also has some negative effects. The increasing use of IT has led to an increase in cyber crimes, where cyber criminals can steal information, disclose secrecy, and spread viruses, thus disturbing social peace and security.

2. Information Security

Information security refers to the process of protecting network infrastructure from unauthorized access, misuse, modification, destruction, or any malfunction. In a network or cyberspace, there is always the threat of cyberattacks, which may result in the loss or damage of precious data. Preventive measures, both physical and software-based, should be adopted to protect computers from malicious activities. Information security creates a secure environment for computers, users, and programs.

There are two key concepts in information security:

  • Security Threats: Possible dangers that may cause unwanted activities in a computer system, leading to a security breach.
  • Security Attacks: Attempts to gain unauthorized access to destroy, expose, steal, or alter valuable data. These attacks are triggered by malicious applications such as viruses, spyware, Trojan horses, and worms.

Information security is built around three major objectives:

  • Confidentiality: Ensures that information is only available to authorized individuals and that no one can access or process another's data without permission.
  • Integrity: Ensures the accuracy and completeness of data, meaning that unauthorized tampering of data is prevented.
  • Availability: Ensures that information is available when required by the user, allowing quick access to valuable data at any time.

3. Cryptography

Cryptography is derived from the Greek word meaning “Secret writing.” It is the art and science of hiding information to provide security for communication. It is mainly used to provide secure communication between individuals, government agencies, and military forces. Cryptography includes two main processes: Encryption and Decryption.

  • Encryption: The process of converting plaintext (a readable message) into an unreadable format (ciphertext) using a special algorithm and key. This ensures that anyone attempting to read the message cannot understand it.
  • Decryption: The reverse process of encryption, which converts the unreadable ciphertext back into the original readable message using a key.

4. What is Digital Divide?

The Digital Divide refers to the gap between those who are computer literate and those who are not. It highlights the division between technical and non-technical individuals in the field of Information Technology (IT). While IT is a modern technology that requires knowledge of computers, many people lack basic computer skills. This gap is known as the Digital Divide. The term gained prominence in the mid-1990s through political speeches and media coverage. This division is widening, especially between urban and rural populations, the educated and uneducated, and the rich and poor. The Digital Divide has become a global issue, and governments are striving to bridge this gap.

5. What are Computer Ethics or Cyber Ethics?

Cyber Ethics, or computer ethics, are the set of moral principles or codes of conduct that computer professionals should strictly follow to avoid criminal activities in computing. Some common examples of computer ethics include:

  • Do not use other people's passwords.
  • Do not steal others' information.
  • Do not use computers to harm others.
  • Do not disclose others' secrets.
  • Do not spread viruses in the network.
  • Do not copy licensed software without paying.
  • Do not alter others' data.
  • Do not take possession of others' intellectual property.
  • Do not misuse electronic transactions.
  • Do not use computers to bear false witness.

6. What is Cyber Crime? List out some examples of Cyber Crimes.

Cyber crime refers to the act of misusing computers, the Internet, or other electronic media for criminal purposes. With the increasing use of Information Technology, cyber crime is also on the rise. Some examples of cyber crimes include:

  • Misuse of computers
  • Spreading viruses
  • Stealing others' information
  • Pirating licensed software
  • Disclosing others' secrets
  • Misuse of electronic transactions and media
  • Making rude and vulgar comments on the Internet
  • Alteration of data without permission
  • Spying on others' information
  • Cracking or hacking
  • Password guessing
  • Salami saving and data diddling
  • Forgery
  • DOS attacks

7. What is Cyber Law? Define and write some provisions of it.

Cyber Law refers to the laws governing the use of computers, the Internet, and other electronic media. It primarily deals with the misuse of technology used for information flow. While cyber ethics suggest the avoidance of cyber crimes, Cyber Law strictly defines punishments for violations of the law. Cyber Law is in practice to support cyber ethics, and the Government of Nepal has enacted the "Electronic Transaction Ordinance, 2005" (11th Falgun, 2061, or 18th March 2005).

Some key provisions of Cyber Law include:

  • Electronic and Digital Signature Law
  • Intellectual Property Laws
  • Copyright Law
  • Trademark Law
  • Semiconductor Law
  • Patent Law
  • Telecommunication Law
  • Data Protection and Privacy Law

8. Explain the different objectives of IT Policy of Nepal.

The IT policies of Nepal include:

  • Declare IT as a high-priority sector for the government and make it practical.
  • Establish knowledge-based industries to increase employment.
  • Make public information and service delivery inclusive and effective through the philosophy of e-government.
  • Increase the productive use of IT in social, economic, and business organizations.
  • Increase access to IT-related international initiatives and developments.
  • Make the Government Integrated Data Center (GIDC) effective to develop as infrastructure for IT development.
